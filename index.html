An overview of our Technology Student Association chapter

Introduction

The Ithaca High School Technology Student Association—IHSTSA—was for a long time overshadowed by Ithaca High School’s other technology clubs. However, for reasons that no one entirely understands, IHSTSA’s membership exploded in September 2015. Though we have always done well at the New York State Competition, this year we needed a long paragraph to list our awards. Gone are the times when we sent only eight members to Nationals—this year, we will send 20 and not everyone will get to go.

Back home in Ithaca, we make up a significant fraction of Ithaca High School’s technology and science geeks:

Community Service

Ithaca High School TSA believes that our primary mission is to educate students about technology’s potential and the allure of being fluent in it. We believe that we can do the most utilitarian good in the world through this education, not through other means. As a result, we typically pay less attention to community service than one might expect of a TSA chapter.

This isn’t to say that we ignore it. On September 12th, members of Ithaca High School TSA assisted as volunteers for the AIDS Ride for Life, a 100-mile bike race around Cayuga Lake that is also a fundraiser for money used to combat AIDS.

Ithaca High School TSA members spent four hours directing bikers so they could navigate correctly—in weather that alternated between pouring rain and a steady drizzle.

During early April, the IHSTSA officers journeyed to a local elementary school that was putting on presentation of technology for elementary schoolers around Ithaca. The officers brought a copy of the State-winning Children’s Stories Submission A Needle in a Haystack, some awards, an easy-to-complete technology problem solving challenge, and the State-winning . Elementary schoolers loved to fire plastic golf balls from the catapult, and the officers hoped they’d garnered a few future recruits to TSA.

In past years, we have always taken a day to trudge through the creek behind our school and pick up trash. We’ve found every type of junk from candy bar wrappers and plastic bags to that of the more exotic sort.

Fundraising

With over 20 members eager to attend nationals, Ithaca High School TSA needs to raise thousands of dollars to pay for a bus to take ourselves, luggage, and great hopes for success to Nashville, Tennessee, for the Technology Student Association national competition. Much of the roughly $650 cost per student will be paid for by parents—but to offset this as much as possible, we see Gertrude Hawk chocolate bars to our fellow students for $1 per bar. Our most successful sellers have made hundreds of dollars on this.

Submissions We’re Really Proud of:

The “Unusual” Catapult

“They changed the rules! We don’t need to have a lever anymore! [Mwahahaha]”

This set four of our officers—Tristan, Zak, Jacob, and Freya—on a course to competing at the New York State Technology Student Association Catapult competition with a catapult modeled on those used to launch planes from aircraft carriers. By which the four really meant air cannon. The catapult’s “unusual” design and ability to make loud tooting noises attracted a lot attention at States. A fiasco resulting from the event rules being unclear caused the four to make a new barrel from duck tape, paper, and hot glue and install it only a few seconds before their turn to compete came. The barrel was also incompatible with the gravity-fed loading magazine that allowed for the planned rapid-fire mode.

Even with all the planned advantages of the “unusual” design taken away, the four were still able to handily win the state competition. Sitting on their laurels, the device was the primary attraction when the IHSTSA officers went to promote technology at a local elementary school. The officers made sure all the children got a turn to fire the catapult—which was difficult, because it was so fun. And to be honest, aging doesn’t make pushing a small red button that makes a loud toot and sends three or four plastic golf balls 20 feet any less amazing.

Flight

Andrew was always quiet and studious. He also didn’t come to IHSTSA meetings often. So everyone was surprised when he brought a featherweight airplane to the New York State Technology Student Association Flight event, launched it, and it didn’t come down—for about two minutes. Andrew easily won first place, and IHSTSA members spent some time afterwards proposing increasingly strong ways to protect Andrew’s submission on its way to Nationals.

This year, a team of the same core people is coming back with a vengeance with A Needle in a Haystack, a story about a girl who designs a complicated contraption to find her lost sewing needle.
